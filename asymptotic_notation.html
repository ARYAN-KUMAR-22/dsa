Current Insight

Asymptotic notation ignores constant-scale details and focuses on what happens as n keeps growing.

Big-O

f(n)=O(g(n)) means f grows no faster than a constant multiple of g for large n.

  • The ratio f/g usually shrinks toward 0 or stays bounded.
  • It is an upper-bound style statement.

Big-Omega

f(n)=Omega(g(n)) means f eventually stays at least as large as a constant multiple of g.

  • The ratio f/g tends to grow or stay away from 0.
  • It is a lower-bound style statement.

Big-Theta

f(n)=Theta(g(n)) means both an upper bound and a lower bound hold.

  • The ratio f/g stabilizes near a positive constant.
  • Constant multipliers do not change the class.

Ratio Intuition

Evaluating f(n)/g(n) at larger checkpoints gives a practical feel for asymptotic behavior.

  • Ratio to 0 suggests Big-O.
  • Ratio to a positive constant suggests Big-Theta.
  • Ratio increasing suggests Big-Omega.

Comparison Pseudocode

pick functions f and g
for each larger n:
    left = f(n)
    right = g(n)
    ratio = left / right
observe ratio trend
if ratio -> 0:
    conclude f is O(g)
if ratio -> c > 0:
    conclude f is Theta(g)
if ratio grows:
    conclude f is Omega(g)

Why Constants Disappear

2n, 5n, and 100n all belong to Theta(n) because asymptotic notation keeps the long-run growth shape and ignores constant scaling.
